Step 1

Describe

Natural language input. No forms, no categories, no structured data. Just describe what you need or what you offer.

Resonance doesn't ask you to fit your intent into a taxonomy someone else designed. There are no dropdowns, no checkboxes, no "select a category." You write a sentence — or a paragraph — in your own words, describing what you're looking for or what you can provide.

// Examples of natural language input "I need a plumber in Barcelona who speaks Catalan" "Frontend developer available for freelance React work" "Looking for a co-founder for an open-source climate project" "I offer piano lessons for beginners, evenings and weekends" "Need someone to help me move a couch on Saturday"

The system understands semantic meaning, not keywords. "I need a plumber" and "my sink is broken and I need help fixing it" resolve to similar vector representations because the AI model understands the underlying intent. This means you don't need to guess the "right" words — you just describe your reality.

Why natural language?

Categories create silos. Natural language captures the full nuance of human needs. A request like "I need someone who understands both accounting and sustainable agriculture" doesn't fit neatly into any category tree — but it embeds perfectly into a continuous vector space where similar intents cluster naturally.

Step 2

Embed

Your device runs an AI model locally to convert your text into a 768-dimensional mathematical vector. No cloud. No API call. Nothing leaves your machine.

The embedding model — Nomic-embed-text — runs entirely on your device using the ONNX runtime. It takes your natural language text and produces a 768-dimensional vector that captures the semantic meaning of what you wrote. Similar meanings produce similar vectors, measured by cosine similarity.

// Your text is converted to a mathematical representation "I need a plumber in Barcelona" ↓ local AI model (Nomic-embed-text v1) [0.0234, -0.1121, 0.0873, 0.0412, -0.0558, ... ] × 768 dimensions
💻

Fully Local

The model runs in a single forward pass on your CPU. No GPU required, no internet connection needed, no data sent to any server. Your text never touches a network interface.

⚡

14ms p95 Latency

Embedding a typical sentence takes about 14 milliseconds at the 95th percentile on consumer hardware. Fast enough to be imperceptible. Measured across 500+ eval runs.

🌐

Multilingual

Nomic-embed-text supports multiple languages. Cross-language similarity (e.g., EN-ES) averages 0.476. Same-language pairs in Spanish and Catalan average 0.650.

Technical Detail

The model weighs approximately 137 MB and runs via ONNX runtime. It's downloaded once during initialization and cached locally. No ongoing network dependency. The 768-dimensional output is L2-normalized before any further processing.

Step 3

Protect

Calibrated Laplace noise is added to every dimension of your embedding before anything leaves your device. This is differential privacy — mathematical, not policy-based.

Before your embedding vector is published to the relay network, your device adds carefully calibrated random noise to each of its 768 dimensions. The noise follows a Laplace distribution, and its magnitude is controlled by the privacy parameter ε (epsilon). This provides formal ε-differential privacy guarantees: even with unlimited computation, an attacker cannot reliably reconstruct your original text from the noisy vector.

// The protection formula scale = 0.38 / (ε · √768) // Each dimension gets independent Laplace noise for i in 0..768: noisy[i] = original[i] + Laplace(0, scale)

Privacy Levels

You choose your privacy level. The trade-off is straightforward: more noise means stronger privacy but fewer matches. Less noise means better matching but a slightly more visible signal.

Level Epsilon (ε) Similarity Retained Match Recall Recommended Use
Low Privacy 5.0 ~99.4% ~98% Non-sensitive content. Maximum matching accuracy.
Medium Privacy 1.0 ~88.1% 82–86% Default. Balanced privacy and utility for most use cases.
High Privacy 0.1 ~18.2% ~14% Highly sensitive needs. Maximum privacy, significantly fewer matches.
This isn't a policy. It's math.

Differential privacy is a mathematical guarantee, not a company promise. The noise is calibrated so that the probability of any output is nearly the same whether or not your specific input was included. No amount of post-hoc analysis, machine learning, or brute-force computation can undo the noise. The privacy parameter ε quantifies exactly how much privacy you get — and you control it.

Step 4

Match

Your perturbed vector is sent to the relay network. The relay runs an HNSW index and cross-matches needs against offers — never same-type. Threshold: 0.50 cosine similarity.

The relay maintains two separate vector indices: one for needs and one for offers. When a new need arrives, the relay searches only the offers index, and vice versa. This cross-matching is fundamental to how Resonance works — it finds complementary people, not duplicates.

// Cross-matching logic if (entry.type === 'need') { matches = offersIndex.search(entry.vector, k=10); } else { matches = needsIndex.search(entry.vector, k=10); } // Only matches above threshold are returned return matches.filter(m => m.similarity >= 0.50);

What the Relay Sees vs. Doesn't See

The Relay Sees The Relay Does NOT See
Perturbed 768-dim vector (noisy) Your original text
Your DID (pseudonymous identifier) Your name, email, phone, or location
Entry type (need or offer) Your true embedding vector
Timestamp of publication Channel messages between matched peers
Cosine similarity scores What you share during progressive disclosure
Important

The relay is stateless and interchangeable. It holds noisy vectors and DIDs — nothing else. Even a malicious relay operator cannot reconstruct your text, link your DID to your real identity, or read your encrypted channel messages. These are structural constraints, not policies.

Step 5

Connect

Both parties are notified of the match. A consent handshake initiates an E2E encrypted channel where true embeddings are exchanged and progressive disclosure begins.

When a match is found, both parties receive a notification containing only the similarity score and the match type. No personal information is exchanged yet. What happens next requires explicit consent from both sides.

Consent Handshake & Key Exchange

If both parties accept the match, an ephemeral X25519 key exchange establishes a shared secret. This secret derives the symmetric key for XSalsa20-Poly1305 authenticated encryption. The channel is end-to-end encrypted — the relay cannot read any messages, even during the pilot phase where channels are relay-bridged to avoid NAT traversal issues.

// Channel establishment const ephemeralKeypair = generateX25519Keypair(); const sharedSecret = diffieHellman(ephemeralKeypair.secret, peerPublicKey); const channelKey = deriveKey(sharedSecret); // All messages encrypted with XSalsa20-Poly1305 const encrypted = secretbox(message, nonce, channelKey);

True Embedding Confirmation

Once the encrypted channel is open, both parties exchange their true, unperturbed embeddings. The receiving side computes cosine similarity between the true embeddings. If similarity exceeds 0.55 (higher than the relay threshold of 0.50), the match is confirmed as genuine. This step catches false positives introduced by the differential privacy noise.

Key Principle

Every step requires your explicit action. The system defaults to silence. Information flows outward only when you push it — the protocol never pulls it from you. If you decide at any point that the match isn't right, you simply stop. No data has been leaked, no profile has been built, no trace remains.
